    Hi! My name is Aditya, and I'm currently trying to get on my way to becoming an ethical hacker of some sort.
    But why?

    To understand that, we need to step back in time.
    Let's go back to 8th grade science class, that magical time when the classwork wasn't actually hard (just somewhat time consuming) and I spent my spare time pushing the limits of Android's freedom instead of doing my homework.

    I remember this day pretty clearly—the bell had just rang, the teacher wasn't here, and she didn't have any work assigned. As you could imagine, nobody really did anything; some were talking to each other, some were playing Fortnite on their school laptops, and some were playing cricket in the back with crumpled tape and a tube of wrapping paper. They kinda sucked, but that's besides the point—it was funny. Meanwhile, I had just recently discovered Termux, the Linux terminal for Android. I'd installed a Kali Linux chroot environment in Termux on my Samsung Galaxy A12, and I was trying to install gobuster. The end goal was to (somehow) do a HackTheBox room entirely on my phone in that 45 minutes.

    Spoiler: it took me about that long just to figure out what gobuster was actually supposed to do. This was before the days where I could ask ChatGPT or Claude about what the heck 'subdomain enumeration' meant, so I was kind of on my own with that one.

    From that day on, I spent the greater half of Q4 in 8th grade doing random easy HTB boxes, purely because it made me feel really cool—I was actually hacking stuff, and I actually understood a lot of what I was doing. When other kids asked me what I was wasting my time on, I could tell them that I was quite literally hacking some machine on the internet because I was bored.

    It was around that time that I decided that security was actually pretty dang interesting, and perhaps something worth pursuing.
    Although the start of the high school workload ate most of my time and I wasn't messing around on HTB at all anymore, there was always that sense of intrigue surrounding the fact that I could literally get paid to be a hacker—legally too—and even learn most of what I needed with sheer will and the vast internet.

    And so it began.
